Rich nations do not always lead in citations. Across five linked visualizations, we trace how macroeconomic indicators, publication quality, field dynamics, international co-authorship, and domestic institutional networks reveal a persistent gap between economic power and knowledge output.

Each figure below is fully interactive. Click any chart to open the expanded view with filters, year controls, and linked detail panels. Use Present mode (P) for a slide-style walkthrough.

I. Countries cluster by more than GDP

High-dimensional indicators — R&D spend, researcher density, citation impact — compress into peer groups that do not align neatly with income tiers. The scatter below projects nations into a shared embedding space.

II. Publication quality shifted faster than quantity

The global mix of elite (Q1) versus lower-tier (Q4) journals has evolved unevenly. Stacked distributions show how quality composition changed decade by decade.

III. Research fields rise and fall like empires

Topic-level publication counts reorder dramatically across decades. The bar chart race surfaces which disciplines dominated each era.

IV. International ties buy a citation premium

Domestically authored papers systematically underperform internationally co-authored work in median citations. The dumbbell plot quantifies this collaboration gap by region.

V. One country, two research economies

India's domestic higher-education network exposes a split between elite institutes and state-affiliated institutions — collaboration density, funding tiers, and geographic hubs tell a layered story.

Methods & data lineage

All figures draw on publicly documented APIs and curated institutional registries. ETL pipelines normalize country codes, journal quartiles, and affiliation tiers before rendering. Module 5 tier statistics reflect verification against NIRF published ranks; edge weights below the top-300 threshold are excluded from year-slice views.
